The LinkedUp Challenge

A series of three competitions promoting the innovative use of linked and open data in an educational context.

There is a wealth of useful and inspirational material on the internet that can be used in education, ranging from lecture slides, tutorials, and online courses to Wikipedia articles and YouTube videos. But how could one find and connect the resources that learners need?

The LinkedUp challenge is all about finding ways to link and mash up educational and cross-domain linked and open data to provide novel applications and services for open and distance education.

There will be three consecutive competitions, and the first one is open now! You can join in at each separate competition; participants to earlier competitions will be able to benefit from support and feedback from the LinkedUp team.


  • First competition: Veni (March 2013 - October 2013): Innovative prototypes and tools for analysing and integrating open educational Web data
  • Second competition: Vidi (November 2013 - April 2014): Challenging and innovative, mature data-driven applications
  • Third competition: Vici (May 2014 - November 2014): Robust applications for large-scale educational use-cases, offered and provided by LinkedUp

To support the challenge, the LinkedUp support action has been collecting and cataloguing data explicitly related to education, ranging from open educational resources to course programmes and educational facilities. The catalogue is available now but will be further refined and extended. There is also a data endpoint, associated with search and exploration services around this data.

These data services are part of the complete environment that the LinkedUp project will provide, including code samples, use case documentation, code clinics and developers' forum.

Join the First Competition: Veni


The goal of the first LinkedUp competition is to gather innovative and robust prototypes for tools that analyse and/or integrate large scale, open web data for educational purposes. Submissions should demonstrate and promote the benefit of open web data technologies for education in the widest sense of the word.

The LinkedUp Veni Competition will be open for any kind of end-user application that analyses and/or makes use of Linked and/or Open Data for any kind of online learning.

How to participate


  • Create a demo, prototype or application that analyses and/or makes use of linked data for any kind of online learning.
  • In your application, you use data sets from the LinkedUp data repository and/or data from other sources.
  • Provide information about your application in several evaluation criteria, such as intended audience and the usability of your application. This will be asked on the submission form.
  • Write an abstract of about 200 words describing your application and its goals. This abstract will be published on the LinkedUp website after acceptance.
  • The demo should be available to the review panel. A publicly available demo or even open source code would be a plus, but is not required.
  • The submission form will launch in April. Please stay tuned and register your interest in the meantime.

Data and development support


To support the challenge, the LinkedUp support action is collecting and cataloguing data explicitly related to education, as well as related data that may be relevant. This includes data about open educational resources, course programmes and educational facilities. Related data may included data about useful Web media, user-generated content, Web lectures or academic publications. The results from the intial efforts focus on explicitly educational data. The Linked Education catalogue is available, as well as through a data endpoint, where a SPARQL endpoint provides access to VoID descriptions of currently included datasets. We are working on additional search and exploration services which will be provided.


Browse The Data


Through the Linked Education data repository, as well as upcoming development support facilities including code samples, use case documentation, code clinics and a developers forum, the LinkedUp support action will provide a complete environment to ensure great, highly exploitable applications are generated from putting together the best developers with all the data they need to create new educational services.

The data and support infrastructure are a continuous work in progress and updates are provided as we go. We are in particular planning substantial updates/releases according to the following schedule.
  • end-March 2013: Expanded Linked Education catalogue with significant amount of OER metadata exposed as linked data. Launch of the “LinkedUp DevTalk” blog/forum.
  • end-April 2013: Release of the Linked Education tagset for cataloguing education-related datasets, and inclusion of datasets useful for student modelling. Opening of the code clinic environment.
  • end-May 2013: Release of advanced interfaces to the catalogue, including search and navigation facilities, and inclusion of datasets regarding statistics in education, courses and academic programmes.



Review process and selection of winners



After the submission deadline, all eligible submissions will be evaluated and rated by the review committee.

Review and rating criteria include data quality, innovative aspects, performance, usability, intended audience and (the lack of) privacy issues.

During the presentation and award event at OKCon in September, the audience will vote the winners from the submissions that received the highest review ratings.

Open data applied to education



LinkedUp seeks to explore and exploit the wealth of web data for education. This includes data with an explicit educational purpose, as well as other data and information that may not have an explicit educational remit, but can usefully be applied to an educational context.

Linked data principles and techniques are used successfully to expose and link datasets from a large variety of resources, covering government, media, life sciences and user-generated content. The cloud of linked data is rapidly growing. Still, there is much more potential for users and society to benefit from widespread access to openly available data.

Education is an area that is expected to benefit highly from linked data approaches. Many institutes offer Open Educational Resources (OER) online. Famous examples include MIT Open CourseWare and the UK Open University's OpenLearn. Linked data makes it possible to connect the various offerings with one another and with general-purpose resources such as Wikipedia.

The field of Learning Analytics focuses on the use of intelligent data, learner-produced data and analysis models to discover information and social connections, and to predict and advise on learning. In the LinkedUp challenges we highly appreciate findings, aggregations, visualizations and other results that pave the way to novel tools that make use of open educational and general-purpose Web resources.
