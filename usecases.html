“How can teachers in the developing world benefit from the open resources freely available online?“

Teaching going social

Proposer

The Commonwealth of Learning (COL) is an intergovernmental organization created by the Commonwealth Heads of Government to encourage the development and sharing of open learning and distance education knowledge, resources and technologies. COL is helping developing nations improve access to quality education and training.

The Commonwealth is a voluntary association of more than 50 independent sovereign states, which provide support to each other, and work together toward international goals.

Context

The use case addresses educational challenges in developing countries, with limited money to invest in education, but a strong need for high quality learning materials.

COL’s work and research suggests that most of higher education students have internet access at their colleges, and half of them at home as well. They are proficient in English, and skilled at manipulating social media tools such as YouTube. They usually use and discuss OERs on social media channels, while many of their teachers do not use or fully appreciate the value of such tools.

Problem

Teachers can access a potentially huge amount of OERs from the internet but it takes time to analyze and evaluate each resource one by one to judge if and how much they fit a learning programme. What could be beneficial for them would be the ability to extract information about the usefulness and attractiveness of learning materials from social media , including learners’ activities, such as rating, liking, and commenting.

The need highlighted by this use case is the one to leverage social media in order to establish the true relevance and value of a resource by analysing the personalized, enriched educational content of social learners. This would allow a first screening of learning materials and would simplify teachers’ evaluation of resources. This simplified evaluation should be based mainly on three parameters:

  • location of information
  • educational value
  • social appreciation

Data sets

The data sets analyzed in this use case are comprised of learner inputs on OERs posted to YouTube and Facebook in the form of ratings, likes and comments. These inputs can be parsed and aggregated to establish a measure of value for each resource and its utility for learners. The educational resources will be provided via the Directory of Open Educational Resources. Subject matter will be full courses at UG level in areas of physical sciences, engineering and technology.

Solutions

We invite applicants to submit prototypes addressing extraction and usage of information from social media, such as mash-ups to

  • Enrich an rss feed from courses space with social information, such as comments from social media (eg. starting with Facebook and Youtube)
  • Provide recommendations for sequencing recommendation what to watch next, or playlists (manual or automatic)
  • Any other innovative idea that connects multiple data sources

Educationizer

Proposer

This use case combines ideas, interests and needs expressed by Jake Berger. Jake works in BBC Archive Development as Programme Manager for Digital Public Space.

At the same time also the project partner Elsevier expressed very similar interests. Elsevier is a world-leading provider of scientific, technical and medical information products and services. The company works in partnership with the global science and health communities to publish more than 2,000 journals, including The Lancet and Cell, and close to 20,000 book titles, including major reference works from Mosby and Saunders. Elsevier also provides several online solutions such as ScienceDirect and Scopus.

The fact that similar needs and interests were expressed by so different and relevant parties strengthens the relevance of the use case.

Context

Most data and media is not explicitly ‘educational’, but much of it has potential for use in education and research if surfaced in the relevant educational context, and described using terms that are recognised and meaningful to the user.

Let’s imagine an instructor who would like to create a course curriculum using open Web data to address an emerging interdisciplinary topic. She opens the Educationizer web app and types a few subject of her curriculum-specific categories into the search field. The app retrieves numerous relevant results, from a range of linked data sets, and clusters them into categories that she recognises, and that are aligned with her course needs, having ‘mapped’ the unfamiliar categories to ones that she can work with.

The material would include content and training materials from European non-profits, open data sets, statistics, published reports, and open access journal articles, as well as media from YouTube, Flickr, Pinterest and structured data from DBPedia, Wikipedia etc.

Problem

The aim here is to support and facilitate the educational (re-)use of informative and multimedia material not originally created for instructional purposes. The challenge is that the majority of data never has a chance to be used as an educational resource. There are two main reasons for this. Firstly it's the sheer volume of data and media available - whether in unstructured form or hard to find, or structured and findable but using vocabularies and knowledge frameworks that are not familiar to educators.

Data sets

Interesting data sets under this perspective are clearly large informative databases and applications, such as wikipedia, dbpedia, other linked open data sets, Youtube, Twitter hashtags, atlas, etc...

Examples include, but are certainly not limited to:

  • http://www.wikipedia.org/
  • http://wiki.dbpedia.org/Datasets
  • http://taggalaxy.de/
  • http://atlas.metabroadcast.com/
  • http://www.flickr.com/services/api/
  • https://developers.google.com/youtube/getting_started#data_api
  • http://www.open.edu/openlearn/

Solutions

With respect to the use case presented here, we are interested into prototypes and applications supporting:

  • mapping non-educationally focussed data (e.g. wikipedia, DBpedia, Youtube, etc...) to structured educational topics
  • vocabulary translation from unfamiliar native vocabularies to those that are recognisable to educators, researchers and learners
  • other and similar facilities that enhance the educational (re-)use of large heterogeneous sets of data.

Simplify

Proposer

This use case combines ideas, interests and needs expressed by: Jake Berger. Jake works in BBC Archive Development as Programme Manager for Digital Public Space

Context

Learners come in all flavours and levels of ability and experience, and everybody begins life knowing nothing. People want to understand things but are sometimes simply too new to the subject to have learned lots of domain-specific words, or even simply too young to have learned enough words at all. Learning should try to be as accessible and attractive to the novice as it is to the expert.

Problem

Most research is by definition domain-specific and written up by experts. Seemingly impenetrable ideas are sometimes possible to understand if the words used are simpler and less domain specific. Can we retain rigor and depth whilst increasing accessibility? Is it easier to translate longer yet simpler explanations? At what level of simplification do the words used make semantic analysis and association easier and more effective (via forced equivalence), and at what level does it become harder (through increased ambiguity)?

Data sets

Sector- and domain-specific datasets, such as databases of scientific research publications. Next to the public LinkedUp data repository, challenge participants are eligible to apply for access to non-public corpora provided by LinkedUp consortium partners and affiliated organisations. Access will be granted for research purposes only and decisions will be made on a case-to-case basis.

Solutions

We are looking for simplifier applications that:

  • using a combination of automated and manual processes (computers first, brains second), analyse and summarise complex abstracts and research in simpler terms - possibly a ‘hierarchy of simplicity’ to create ‘versions’ for different ages and abilities
  • make research papers more easily readable by less-advanced learners and easier to auto-translate
  • link difficult words to wiktionary / wikipedia, offering wiki-style crowdsourcing of improvements.

Supporting employees’ development via didactic guidance through learning materials

Proposer

This use case originates from consortium discussions and brainstorming. It represents actual needs of SMEs and SMEs employees. It also describes themes and issues that are frequently encountered in discussions about MOOCs.

Context

At a company, employees may wish, or be encouraged by the company itself, to attend online courses to improve their knowledge, keep up-to-date and have the opportunity to progress in their career.

Many open, university-level courses and resources are available nowadays, including the popular Massive Open Online Courses (MOOCs). The Company would appreciate some way to ensure that employees could follow a meaningful structure across worthwhile, helpful courses.

Under this perspective, a certification is not strictly required, but it would be nice to have some way to track these activities as well.

Problem

A plethora of online courses and MOOCs is freely available; learners need to search and select by themselves the ones who seem to better suit their needs. What would be helpful here is a guidance system, i.e. to suggest which course would make sense to take next or to work on simultaneously; and/or basing on own learning/career objectives, previous learning (prerequisites), etc.

Another valuable support would be that of remediation to relearn and reflection. Another useful support would be a kind of formal recording / history of attended MOOC, providing some sort of progression timeline, or updated resume.

The kind of support and guidance described above would be beneficial both for the individual employee’s learning and obviously, in turn, the Company itself.

Data sets

Many institutions and organizations provide MOOCs, such as Coursera, edX, Udacity, etc...

Solutions

In respect to this use case, we look forward for your proposals for support and guidance across MOOCs and OERs, i.e. to suggest which course would make sense to take next, or to work on simultaneously, basing on:

  • the employee’s own objectives
  • learning history and actual knowledge (prerequisites)
  • remediation to (re)learn
  • reflection on past learning


Furthermore, we are also interested in more crowd-based search and structuring facilities of existing offerings (journals, conferences, books, etc…) and new online tools.

Apps

From a previous challenge, we have gathered some of the best apps that people built around linked data for education:

Cite4Me

Semantic Retrieval and Analysis of Scientific Publications

Cite4Me interlinks the dataset with related data sources from the Linked Open Data cloud, and provides innovative search, visualization, retrieval and recommendation of scientific publications from the dataset and related interlinked resources.

GNOSS

Applications for Learning Analytics Research

Exploitation of Linked Data about learning analytics research through innovative end-user applications built on GNOSS, a semantic and social software platform. It allows users to find and discover new information. The featured applications are: faceted searches, enriched contexts, navigation through graphs and graphic visualization in charts or geographic maps.

Topic dynamics

A Dynamic Topic Model of Learning Analytics Research

Visual analytics of topic dynamics in the dataset between 2008 and 2012. The data set was processed using probabilistic, dynamic topic mining algorithms. This application is based on D-VITA, a web-based browsing tool for dynamic topic models.

Paperista

Visual Exploration of Semantically Annotated Research Papers

The visualization and exploration tool Paperista presents topics of publications in the form of bubble and line charts. The tool provides multiple views, thus allowing users to observe and interact with topics, understand their evolution and relationships over time, and compare data originating from different research fields (i.e., LA and EDM).
